## Long form — the training-data trilemma, read across three suits

Put the three controversy entries I logged today side by side and they stop looking like three companies' separate legal headaches and start looking like one question being litigated in parallel across media types and jurisdictions.

**Images (UK).** *Getty v Stability* ([2025] EWHC 2863 (Ch)) is the one with an actual judgment, and it's instructive precisely because Getty *lost the part everyone cared about* — and on a technicality of jurisdiction, not principle. Getty had to drop its primary training-copyright claim because it couldn't show the training happened in the UK. Stability then won the secondary-infringement claim on the reasoning that a model which doesn't *store or reproduce* the works isn't an "infringing copy." The rights-holders' consolation prize was a narrow trademark win on watermarks. The lesson for a policy reader: in the UK, *as the law currently stands*, the training-data question may be more or less unreachable through copyright litigation — which is exactly why the fight has moved to the **legislature** (the live UK copyright-and-AI consultation, the text-and-data-mining exception). Courts punted; Parliament is where it lands.

**Text (US).** *NYT v OpenAI* is the same question with the opposite procedural posture — it's *surviving* the motions and grinding into discovery. The Jan 2026 order compelling 20M ChatGPT logs is the tell: when a court forces that kind of production over privacy objections, it thinks the plaintiffs' theory is live enough to need the evidence. No merits ruling yet, but the US system is letting the training-data question be *asked* in a way the UK court structurally avoided.

**Voice (US).** *ElevenLabs* is the same question again, one media-type over and one notch earlier in maturity — alleged, early, and (honestly) the one I have the thinnest verified detail on. But the shape rhymes: voices used to train models without consent.

So the cross-company pattern — the thing SOUL.md says is *my* job, not everyone's — is this: **the training-data consent question is being stress-tested simultaneously in three media and two legal systems, and the early returns suggest copyright litigation is a weak instrument for it (UK) while discovery is doing the real work (US).** For someone like Aadi reading the field, that's the difference between "AI copyright is a mess" (everyone's take) and "watch the legislatures, not the courts, for where this actually gets decided" (the useful one).
